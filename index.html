A Star's Life

Star's life are up to their size as a big star will die only in about a couple hundred thousand years because of how much fuel the star consumes, but smaller stars consume their fuel much more slower, as our sun is one of the many smaller star's. Stars have 7 stages, named Giant gas cloud, Protostar, T-Tauri phase, main sequence, Red giant, fusion heavier of elements, and finally supernovae and Planetary nebulae. Giant gas cloud is when the star starts and the temperature is low enough to use synthesis. Protostar is when gas particles collide, producing heat and making the formation of warm accumulation of molecules that can be seen through infrared. T-Tauri phase is when solids collide into a protostar, releasing a lot of energy, this phase lasting about 100 million years.

Phases of a Star

The main sequence phase is when the star reaches a temperature forcing hydrogen to transform helium, making the star's core so hot it releases, once again, a lot of energy. The Red Giant phase is when the star runs out of hydrogen to transform into helium, becoming a sub-giant then a red giant because of the cooler surface compared to the main sequence's surface. The fusion of heavier elements is when the fusion of helium ends causing fusion of carbon to commence repeating the process until iron forms in the core causing the core to collapse in the core turning into a white dwarf, if a small star like the sun. Supernovae and Planetary nebulae are when a star explodes and all the material flies off into space, but it converts into a neutron star also known as a black hole. However smaller stars will not explode as they turn into white as the solids will drift away slowly, but stars smaller than the sun will not burn anything as their mass is too small but the red glow from the main sequence, these stars can last for trillions of years

There are five types of nebula that can form after a star's death including the Emission Nebula, Reflection Nebula, fairly Dark Nebula, Planetary Nebula and a Supernova Remnant.
